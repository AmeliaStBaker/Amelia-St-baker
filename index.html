The Story

Where wealth meets behaviour.

Amelia St Baker grew up inside one of Australia's most significant family wealth structures. She watched, from the inside, how ultra-high-net-worth families think, communicate, govern, and sometimes fracture. That vantage point became her life's work.

At twenty, Amelia was diagnosed with cancer. That confrontation with mortality, and the recalibration of what truly matters, became the lens through which she now sees everything: leadership, legacy, and why the work you build must outlast you.

An award-winning Occupational Therapist turned entrepreneur, Amelia co-founded Ninja Allied Health, growing it to 40+ staff in under two years and delivering over ten thousand hours of therapy, while committing more than a thousand hours of free care annually to people who can't access funding. In 2026, she acquired the Redlands Children's Therapy Centre, a multidisciplinary clinic supporting children and families across South East Queensland.

She completed her MBA at the University of Queensland as the youngest person ever admitted, was named in the InDaily 40 Under 40, and spent two years on the Queensland Government Innovation Advisory Council advising on innovation strategy and emerging technology.

Today, she works with a small number of ultra-high-net-worth families on the behavioural architecture that determines whether wealth survives across generations. She also teaches business to the health cohort at the University of Queensland, covering what most business schools never go near.

The Problem

Generational wealth has a behaviour problem.

The data is unambiguous. Wealth doesn't fail because of bad investments. It fails because of unresolved family dynamics, unprepared heirs, and the absence of a behavioural architecture to hold it together. That's exactly what Amelia's work addresses.
